SA 1 List and briefly describe the four types of requirement errors discussed in the lecture, providing one concrete example for each. 5 pts
[Student writes answer here — approximately 10–15 lines expected]
✓ Model Answer
  • 1. Errors of Omission — Important information is not stated. Example: Writing "This software shall provide cost estimates" when the actual intent covered size prediction for source code and manuals, staffing plans, and schedule estimates across all development activities. The developer assumed they understood what "cost estimates" meant.
  • 2. Errors of Commission — Wrong information, contradiction, or both. Example: Req #1 says "proof of valid insurance must be provided before issuing vehicle registration" and Req #2 says "vehicle registration information is required before issuing proof of insurance" — a logical impossibility (chicken-or-egg problem).
  • 3. Errors of Clarity — Requirements stated in a way that leads to confusion due to ambiguous wording. Example: "Commanded pre-task calibration updates offset coefficients only for the corresponding calibration table" — "only" placement changes the entire meaning versus the intended version.
  • 4. Errors of Understanding — Ambiguity leads people to internalize requirements differently and create their own vision. Example: "The ATM shall respond quickly and in a user-friendly manner" — "quickly" and "user-friendly" are undefined; each reader applies their own interpretation. Ambiguity is the fundamental problem: "people internalize requirements, apply their own definitions of words and create their own vision."

(Lecture 2, Slides 1.14–1.19)
SA 2 Explain the difference between Requirements Elicitation, Requirements Analysis, Requirements Specification, and Requirements Verification & Validation. Why is this process described as iterative? 5 pts
[Student writes answer here — approximately 10–15 lines expected]
✓ Model Answer
  • Requirements Elicitation: "The process through which the stakeholders and the developers of a software/system discover, review, articulate, and understand the stakeholders' needs and the constraints on the software/system and the development activity." Techniques include interviews, questionnaires, brainstorming, prototyping, storyboarding, and observation.
  • Requirements Analysis: "The techniques of deciding which features are appropriate for the product based on stakeholder needs." It involves prioritizing needs, uncovering missing/conflicting requirements, and producing requirements models (diagrams, tables, matrices).
  • Requirements Specification: "The techniques of documenting the external behavior of a system that will be built based on the features selected during the analysis process." Produces the Software Requirements Specification (SRS) or Functional Specification document.
  • Verification & Validation: "The techniques of ensuring that a requirements specification meets the stakeholders' needs." Verification checks correctness; validation checks that the right product is being built.
Why iterative? Each step is "an opportunity to engage stakeholders, reduce resistance, and increase probability of project success." Stakeholder needs evolve, new details emerge, and misunderstandings are discovered — requiring the team to loop back and refine earlier steps continuously. (Lectures 4–5, Slides 1.11–1.18)

Scenario — Cafeteria Ordering System (COS) Read Carefully Before Answering

Background: DTU International School is planning to digitize its cafeteria operations. Currently, students stand in long queues to order food, pay by cash, and wait for their meals. Cafeteria staff manage orders manually on paper slips, frequently making errors or running out of popular dishes without warning. The administration has no visibility into sales data, inventory levels, or peak demand times.

The school's senior management wants to commission a Cafeteria Ordering System (COS) — a web-based and mobile application that allows students to pre-order meals, pay digitally, and pick up their food at a designated counter without waiting in line. Kitchen staff must receive real-time orders. The cafeteria manager needs reports on daily sales, item popularity, and stock levels. External payment gateways (e.g., VNPay) will process transactions.

Known constraints:

  • Budget: Fixed. No increase possible.
  • Launch deadline: Start of next academic semester (16 weeks).
  • The system must work on both web browser and mobile (iOS/Android).
  • Must integrate with the school's existing Student Information System (SIS) for authentication.
  • Some students do not own smartphones — they must be able to use kiosk terminals in the cafeteria.

✓ Model Answer — UC03 Use Case Specification
USE CASE SPECIFICATION — UC03: Place Order
Use Case ID
UC03
Use Case Name
Place Order
Description / Summary
This use case describes how a Student (or Kiosk User) selects food items from the menu, reviews their selection, and submits an order to the Cafeteria Ordering System. The system confirms the order and forwards it to the kitchen for preparation.
Actors
Primary: Student, Kiosk User
Secondary: Kitchen Staff (receives order), VNPay (payment — via «include» Make Payment)
Pre-Conditions
  1. The Student is logged into the system (UC01 has completed successfully).
  2. The menu is available and at least one item is in stock.
  3. The system is connected to the internet and the kitchen display is online.
Basic Flow of Events
  1. Student opens the "Menu" section of the application.
  2. System displays available food items with prices, descriptions, and stock status.
  3. Student selects one or more items and specifies quantity for each.
  4. System adds selected items to the Student's cart and displays a running total.
  5. Student clicks "Review Order" to view the cart summary.
  6. Student selects a desired pickup time from available time slots.
  7. Student clicks "Confirm Order."
  8. System invokes «include» Make Payment use case (UC04).
  9. Upon successful payment confirmation, system generates a unique Order ID and confirmation message.
  10. System sends the order details to the Kitchen Display (UC06 — Receive & Process Order is triggered).
  11. Student receives a push notification / on-screen confirmation with Order ID and estimated pickup time.
Alternative Flows
AF-1: Item out of stock
At step 3, if the Student selects an item that is out of stock, the system displays a warning message and prevents adding that item. The system suggests alternative items.

AF-2: Cart is empty
At step 5, if the cart is empty, the system prompts the Student to add at least one item before confirming.

AF-3: Student modifies order
At step 5, the Student may return to the menu (step 2) to add or remove items before confirming.
Post-Conditions
  1. A confirmed order record is stored in the system with status "Pending."
  2. Payment record is created with status "Paid."
  3. Kitchen Staff can view the new order on the Kitchen Display.
  4. Stock levels for ordered items are decremented accordingly.
Special Requirements
  • System must display menu and process order within 3 seconds on standard internet connection.
  • System must support concurrent orders from multiple students without data conflict.
  • User interface must be accessible on mobile (iOS/Android) and web browser.
  • Kiosk terminal version must support touch-screen input.
Exceptions
E-1: Network failure — If the network disconnects during order submission (step 7–9), the system displays an error message and prompts the Student to retry. The order is NOT submitted until a confirmation is received.

E-2: Payment failure — If VNPay returns a failed transaction, the system cancels the order submission and prompts the Student to retry payment or choose another method. Order is not forwarded to kitchen.

E-3: Session timeout — If the Student's session expires during ordering, the system saves the cart state, redirects to login (UC01), and restores the cart upon successful re-authentication.
REF: Lecture 7, Slide 1.39 — "Documenting Use Cases" template; Slide 1.40–1.41 — Summary of use case properties

The lecture states: "Every requirement has risk which is a condition that could endanger the project or the product development. Requirements risk mitigation is a process to assess requirements-related risks and identifies actions to avoid or minimize those risks." (Lecture 3, Slide 1.54)

RiskImpactMitigation Strategy
Risk 1: Incomplete stakeholder involvement — kitchen staff and kiosk users not consulted early enough Requirements may miss critical operational workflows, leading to a system kitchen staff cannot use effectively and kiosk users find confusing. This falls under "Error of Omission" — important use cases would be left out. Apply elicitation guideline step 3: "Identify appropriate representatives from each user class." Conduct structured interviews and observation sessions ("Observing users at work") with kitchen staff during the requirements phase. Develop prototypes and show them to kiosk users for early feedback before the first sprint. Hold a joint JAD (Joint Application Development) session with all stakeholder groups.
Risk 2: SIS integration requirements are unclear or technically infeasible within the budget/timeline If the Student Information System's API is undocumented or restricted, the authentication requirement (UC01) becomes a blocker. This could delay the entire project or force a costly workaround. Immediately schedule a technical feasibility session with the IT Department / SIS Administrator (who is a key stakeholder). Apply the requirement attribute "Feasible: possible to implement." Document integration constraints as explicit requirements risks in the Vision & Scope document under "Business Risks." Identify an alternative authentication method (e.g., student ID + password) as a contingency scope item in the "Scope of Subsequent Release" section.
